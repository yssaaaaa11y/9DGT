Introduction about who they are

Suzanne Aubert was a Catholic sister born in France.
She dedicated more than sixty years to mission work in Aotearoa New Zealand.
In 1892, she founded the Daughters of Our Lady of Compassion.
She left a strong legacu in social welfare, nursing care, and education, especially for Maori communities and those marginalized in early settler society.


Where did they carry out their mission?

Auckland: Arrived in 1860 and began teaching at a boarding school for Maori girls, Nazareth Institute, with the Holy Family Congregation.
Hawke's Bay (Meanee): From around 1871, served at the Marist Maori mission under the Third Order of Mary, She taught, nursed, and built trust between Maori and Pakeha communities.
Hiruharama / Jerusalem (Whanganui River): From 1883, revived the mission there by teaching, farning, nursing, practicing herbal medicine alongside Maori healers, and caring for abandoned children.
This became the birthplace of her own congregation. Wellington: From 1899, opened St Joseph's Home for Incurables, a creche, a soup, and Our Lady's Home of Compassion in Island Bay.
These facilities supported disabled people, the poor, and working families from all faiths.

What are the ways they showed examples of the Marist Pillars

Family Spirit

Suzanne Aubert created a strong sense of whanau wherever she served.
She treated everyone like family, especially the sick, the poor, and abandoned children.
She lived among the people, sharing their struggles and joys,
Her compassing and love made people feel valued and cared for, just like a real family would.

Simplicity

Suzanne lived a humble and simple life.
She didn't care about wealth or status.
She worse plain clothes, ate simplefood, and used her talents to help others.
Her mission was never about being famous but about serving those in need, which shows her dedication to living simply

In the Way of Mary

Suzanne followed Mary's example by saying "yes" to God's call, even when things were tough.
Like Mary, she was gentle, faithful, and full of love.
She worked closely with Maori communities, listened to them, and respected their culture, showing humility and a spirit of a kindness and service.

Love of Work

Suzanne had an incredible work ethic.
She ran homes for orphans and the sick, made medicine using native plants, started a religious order, and even traveled to Rome alone to get permission for her mission.
Her dedication and energy showed her deep love for her work and her calling.
